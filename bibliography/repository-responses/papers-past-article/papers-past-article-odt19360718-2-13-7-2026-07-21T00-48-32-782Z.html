TRAGEDY OF THE PACIFIC

AN ISLAND KINGDOM

" The Phantom Paradise." By J. H. Niau. Illustrated. (Sydney: Angus and Robertson); 7s 6d.

The storv of the colonisation of the islands of "the Pacific is one rich in romance, humour, and pathos. The lure of these sea-girt paradises where white men were little less than gods (with exceptions where a delight in " long pig " outweighed the early wonderment of the natives), and where fortunes accrued without the necessity of labour, excited the imaginations of unscrupulous adventurers as well as irresolute dreamers. The history of our own country records the ignominious failure of one misunderstood Frenchman, Charles, Baron de Thierry, whose grandiose schemes for the establishment of a monarchy in New Zealand disintegrated before the mocking laughter of the Maoris, traders, and missionaries. A parallel to this, but

one which was carried to a more incredible and tragic conclusion in a lonelier corner of the South Seas, was instigated by another Frenchman half a century later, and, for the first time in English, the story of the Marquis de Rays and his ambition of a new France has been written. Josephine Hyacinthe Niau is a daughter of one of the subscribers to the scheme, and her testimony provides an illuminating insight into one of the most heartless and fantastic deceptions practised in the Pacific. Early in his life it had been foretold by a gipsy seer that Charles Bonavenlure du Breil, Marquis de Rays, would be a king. Twice he tried to realise this prophecy by endeavouring to found colonial empires, first at Madagascar and then in Indo-China. Both attempts were failures. In his comfortable Breton home he happened to read the description by Duperry, commander of the Coquillo, of a stay at Port Praslin, at the southern end of New Ireland (now incorporated in the Mandated Territory of New Guinea). By some queer trick of fate —and the destiny and happiness of a thousand people were to be affected by the incident —the weather had been gloriously fine for the nine days Duperry spent there, and he wrote a glowing account of the charm of the harbour. Almost invariably, however, there is uninterrupted rain for eight months of the year in the natural basin. It was here that de Rays declared an empire, La Nouvclle France, with himself, Charles I, at its head. By skilful advertising, and what Americans would describe as " ballyhoo." hundreds of war-weary peasants were induced to part with their scanty savings in the hope that they would be taken to a peaceful and fertile land. The French Government frowned on the project but the Marquis evaded official interference by clearing his ships from Flushing and Barcelona. In four successive shiploads the emierants lighthoartedly set out for the New France. The ships were small

and so badly provisioned that much of the food was uneatable, but the passengers were kept subdued by the tyrannical cruelty of the commanders. Disillusionment was complete when they arrived at Port Praslin (renamed Port Breton). Beautiful it undoubtedly was, with its rocky, bush-clad slopes running down to the sea, but in the whole area—of which " King Charles " had not yet bought an acre—there was only one narrow strip suitable for cultivation. Both Port Breton and Liki-Liki, where others were landed, were infested with malaria. At the latter place, instead of rocks, there was a brackish bog for the farmers to cultivate. Nothing would grow on it. Once on shore the colonists were not allowed to regain the ships, being kept oil' at the point of a revolver. The death roll was enormous. Some were killed by the cannibal natives, who were never far away in the bush, and fever, exposure and starvation continually augmented the row of crosses along the beach, the only spot at Port Breton where graves could be dug. In their few poor houses the people spread umbrellas over their sick families to protect them from the warm rain that poured through the warped roofs. The mud that flowed down the mountain side oozed through the walls and they were afraid to lift the boards of the floor lest they see the snakes, scorpions and poisonous insects they knew linked there. But the "King" was indifferent to the appeals of his despairing " subjects." By " divine right" he conferred the most extraordinary powers upon his commanders. " You may condemn to death anyone who questions your authority'or mine," he wrote on one occasion. " You not only may sentence him to death; it is your absolute duty to do so. should there be the slightest insubordination. Fortunately for the colonists, the last captain to arrive was more humane than his predecessors. By his efforts the remaining sickened subjects were taken from the tropical death-trap, eventually landing at Manila, Noumea, and Sydney. At Manila, where one of the ships and its cargo was sold, some of the " necessaries of pioneering life" which had been sent out by the Marquis were unpacked. Amongst the 400 cases were 2000 dog collars, a Punch and Judy show, and thousands of satin slippers—the latter no doubt for the dusky belles who would grace the court of "Charles I of Oceania." Most of the survivors of the expedition settled in Australia, where some, who were children at the time, still live. In " The Phantom Paradise," Miss Niau has written an historical work which has long been missed. She is bitter, but one can understand her bitterness towards the man who wrecked the happiness of her family. If there is a drawback to the work it is its brevity. In a Volume of this kind, and more particularly because it is the only one m English yet published on the subject, a more detailed description of life on the island would be welcome for the sake of future historians and authors who write against an historical background. A number of interesting illustrations are included in the book. E. A. A,
