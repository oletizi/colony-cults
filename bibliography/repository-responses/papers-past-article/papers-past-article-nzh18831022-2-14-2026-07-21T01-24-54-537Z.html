Death has again thinned the ranks of our earliest colonists. In our obituary notices will be found the announcement of the demise of Mr. John Sullivan, of Mahurangi Heads, whofirstvisited the colonies in the year 1832. Arriving at Hobart in the Vestal, of Hull, proceeding from there to Sydney, and thence to China, he arrived in New Zealand in 1834, and for ten years occupied himself chiefly with coasting affairs. In 1844 he settled at Mahurangi Heads, and resided there until his last sickness brought him to Auckland. He was the owner of a number of vessels trading oh this and the West Coast, and was widely known and much respected for genial qualities and uprightness of character. He leaves a son, William, master of the steamer Kotuku, and a daughter, the wife of Mr. W. B. Jackson, of Mangawhare.

At the late meeting of the Committee of the Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Mr. David Goldie gave some interesting information as to the working of similar societies in Australia, and of the one established in Christchurch. He elated that in Christchurch the work of noting cases of cruelty was not solely left to a paid inspector, but that cards showing the rules and objects of the society were extensively circulated, and that at given points in the town forms wire obtainable, so that when a member of the society or other person saw an act of cruelty to an animal being perpetrated, they could go and fill up one of the forms in the regular way, with the necessary particulars. The form was then forwarded to the inspector, who investigated the ease, obtained evidence, and if necessary prosecuted. On market days he attended the sale yards, to see that no cases of cruelty occurred to stock of the various classes.

An effort is being made by an appeal to the friends of the District Hospital, and the benevolent public generally, to get the wards adorned with some good pictures. Nothing is more depressing to the sick and fevered* than to gaze monotonously, day by day, on the square yards of lath and plaster, and it is believed that by making the surroundings jnore cheerful the mental condition of the ■nek would be improved, and convalesence promoted- Men even in health are greatly influenced by their surroundings,—how much more those who are weak in body, and by sympathy with it, sometimes weakened also in mind. Theodore Parker, speaking on the subject of surrounding influences, said, "No one could look upon Nature and see the annual miracle of spring without being reminded of the Great Miracle-worker, but looking abroad in the great city upon a wilderness of stone and mortar, it was hard to deduce God from a brick pavement!" Some kind friends of the District Hospital have contributed several pictures already, but there is ample room for more—not daubs—but for pictures worthy of the donors and of the object for which they are given.
