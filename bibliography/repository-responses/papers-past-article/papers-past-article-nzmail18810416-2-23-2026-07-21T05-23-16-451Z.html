COLLAPSE OP THE MARQUIS DE RAY’S EXPEDITION.

(Sydney Morning Herald’s New Caledonia Correspondent.) Noumea, March 18. At 4 p.m. on the 12th March a disabled steamer struggled into Noumea harbor, having on board 300 starving souls, with not a drop of water to drink. Such a story as of this vessel has very rarely to be told. Ere commencing its relation let me record a fact that should cover its authors with deep shame. The vessel on arrival was boarded by one of our merchants, who was commissioned to furnish the food required by the people on board—literally in a starving condition. He went to the nearest butcher’s and ordered a quarter of beef, the usual price of which is 90 centimes (9d.), the kilo (2Jlbs.). The price was immediately augmented to 120 centimes. On expostulating against this, the purchaser was inforrr ed that was their price for choice meat ; that in question was a forequarter in a very doubtful state, necessarily cooked immediately on receipt. The invoice was taken on board, with the fact noted that the price had been forced from 90 to 120 centimes ; but the next morning meat would be found elsewhere at the usual price. The ship’s purser had that store pointed out to him at his particular request. The unknown flag shown by the ship was at first taken to be American, but she proved to be the British India S. N. Co.’s India (s.), 900 : tons, Captain Leroy, flying the Liberian colors, Port Breton on her stern, and having on board j the remnant of the Marquis de Ray’s New Ireland colonisation victims, numbering between 250 and 260, including upwards of eighty children, from infancy to the age of fourteen years. When water and food were taken on board, a rush was made by the poor creatures starving for both. The vessel was a horrid spectacle. Finding themselves aban- ’ doned at New Ireland by the Genii, for whioh they looked in vain, the officers of ' the atttempt of the . Marquis de Ray at colonising, and the remnant of his victims, held a consultation, and decided to leave for Noumea whilst they had a few days’

provisions left. “As well to perish at sea as die of starvation, fever, and other miseries on that inhospitable island.” Accordingly, on Fabruary 20th, they embarked everything on board and started for Noumea, having only eleven days’ provisions and fifteen days’ water and a very small quantity of coal. Their machinery proved almost useless, owing to faulty boilers. An occasional five or ten minutes’ work alone could be obtained. At the north end of New Caledonia the greatest diffiulty was experienced in keeping off the reef, 500 yards distant one time, and again, on entering the Pass, the machinery stopped, but a fortuitous breeze came to their aid. The voyage was thus prolonged to twenty instead of ten or twelve days, causing much additional suffering to the already over-afflicted emigrants, seven of whom died ere reaching here, and four the day after. Two hundred and sixty Italians and thirty French started in her from Barcelona eight months since. Of these twentyfour have died. They were from the age of one up to seventy-nine years, hut the majority were men in the prime of life. These poor creatures seem a very respectable lot of people of the agricultural class, in families of from three to four to ten or twelve. Some of their contracts with the Marquis de Ray testify to their having paid for the “privileges” promised them sums varying from 650 to 2000 francs, a large sum for an Italian peasant. Misery and suffering have pursued them from the moment of embarking, when a number found themselves robbed of all their luggage by a rascally agent. Arrived here, efforts were made to induce them to come ashore. Very naturally they have refused to quit the ship, unless with a sure guarantee of their being properly disposed of or returned to their homes. Hived on two decks, having two rows of sufficiently comfort able berths on each, women, children, and men pass away the heavy hours as best they can. What suchanestmustbeatsea in rough weather it is painful to imagine. Kind hearts were not found wanting in helping the most pressing wants of the poor creatures. A subscription was freely responded to, and little comforts taken on board. To see the forest of hands—ten to twenty deep stretched out for the cakes or sweets distributed to the children, the happy looks of the parents ; to hear the “ Gracio, Signor to feel one’s hand taken by an old grey-headed man, and gratefully kissed for the packet of tea just handed to his poor wife, lying helpless on her mattress ; to see the pretty little Italian faces glistening with pleasure as the children received their welcome piece of cake or pudding ; to hear the earnest adieus on leaving, all combined to make an immovable impression on visiting this unfortunate vessel.

The situation of those on board is most embarrasing and difficult to deal with. No money was procurable here upon the insufficient guarantee offered ; the supply of provisions to so large a number of people has been too heavy to be longer continued by tbe merchants. Consequently they have been stopped, and the people thrown upon the humanity of the Government, which is now caring for them awaiting information from Sydney. It is a case in which a Government only can act, for even the wages of the sailors are unpaid. Efforts, especially since a report arrived per mail that the Marquis de Ray is arrested and imprisoned, have been made by the captain to procure money, but in vain ; his guarantee is not deemed sufficient, nor the value of the goods on board, including 4 mitrailleuses and 400 Winchester rifles. Many are of opinion that the emigrants are very wise in sticking to the ship, spite of all inconvenience, and the temptation of a run ashore. The captain s efforts to induce the Government to adopt the people have failed, as a matter of course.
