THE STORY OF THE MARQUIS DE RATS' EXPEDITION.

OITK cablegrams have recently announced that the appeal of the notorious Marquis de Kays has been dismissed by the French Court of AppeaL and that his long deforced trial is at last to take place. The history of this remarkable case, as told by the Pans correspondent of the Sydney Morning Herald, is, therefore, worth repeating at the pre-ent time. Charles Bonaventuro du Breil (Marquis de Rays) is now 50 years of age. He belongs to the old nobility of Brittany, and started in life with some little fortune. In his youth he had a passion for travelling, and spent come time in America and Senegal. Ia 1877 he conceived the project of founding in Oceanica a "free and Christian colony," and it may be that at the outset he was animated by an honest though foolish enthusiasm. The first public announcement of his scheme, destined to entail such disastrous consequences, was contained in an advertisement commencing—"Colonie Libre de PortBreton.—Lands at five francs the hectar.-, payable by one franc per month. A rapid fortune assured without quitting the country." Applications were to be sent to "M. Le Marquis du Breil de Rays. An office was then opened in Pans, and a prospectus was distributed far and wide, the following extract from which throws further light upon the nature of Du Breil s scheme : "The free colony of Port Breton (Oceanica), in the Archipelago of Now Britain, near New Caledonia, under the direction of the Marquis do Rays, Consul for Bolivia in France, has for its object the acquisition of excellent lands, in good farming condition for Europeans desirous of obtaining freehold* 1 colonial estates—free from all risk of interference or political trouble 3." A journal already existing, the Nouvelle France, lent itself immediately to the enterprise, and even became its official orgs.il. It was largely through the instrumentality oE this paper that so many unfortunate people were drawn into du Breil's net. In one of its articles (produced at the

trial), it said " we wish 10 iuuuu iu Oceanica, talving England for our example, a free society for the -raising aud disposal of colonial produae. It will have for its basis a central factory for the preparation of sngar, oils, and preserves, to which will be added the exploitation of timber. Shops attached to the factory will, after the American system, supply tools and other articles of European * manufdeture to the colonists. The securities will have a negotiable value, which will be progressive in proportion to the development of the colony. An admirable prospect is thus offered to the smallest capitalist." To the argument of the Nouvelle France the Marquis added his platform eloquence. He " stumped'"' France to attract subscribers to liis scheme. Religion entered considerably into his method of operations. In one of his speeches he said :—"The conception of our colony originated iu religious and patriotic sentiment. You are aware that every human enterprise attempted in defiance of God is doomed to failure. Faithful sons of our ancient country, let us impress upon our new undertaking that sacred character which will be the life of it—let us give it Catholic baptism. Australia (tic.) alone can offer to our work a field for serious action. To that part of the world, then, let our eyes be turned. The port is found. It is situated in the south eastern

portion of New Ireland, in the great line of maritime traffic between Australia and China. It is Port Breton, now called Port Praslin." Du Breil'a efforts met with such Buccess that from 1577 to ISSO the money paid over to him and his agents amounted to 1.500.000 francs. At first the public would not listen to him, but when he found purchasers for his hectares, he raised the price successively from 5 francs to 10 francs, 20, and 60 francs the hectare. His shrewdness told him that confidence would increase in proportion as the price went up. And 80 it was. Besides his central office in Paris, he had agescies in Havre, Marseilles, Antwerp, Brussels, and Jersey. In addition to the fixed agencies Du Breil employed a number of persons to travel through the country, towns, and villages to excite the cupidity of small capitalists, and at the same time to blow the religious tlimpet so as to work towards the same end through twn distinct though not incompatible channels. By sueh means, before the bubble burst in 18S1, Du Breil had sold 700,000 hectares of land at i°ort Breton, which toge'eher make a vastly larger area than that of the whole island. Although he had never visited the country himself he had, through his emissaries, purchased a portion of the territory of the native chief Maragano at the nominal price of 1550 franc 3. There was, however, no money transaction ; for the chief ■was willing enough to accept as an equivalent tobacco, hatchets, and beads, and a red velvet costume. The document was drawn up by M. Chambaud, a Marseilles notary without practice, whom Du Breil had sent to Port Breton with the title of " President of the Civil Tribunal of New France." Maragano, having put his cross to it, sealed the compact ■with a dance that convulsed with laughter the few Europeans who witnessed theperformance. The number of companies founded for speculative purposes, or presumably so, in connection with Du Breil's scheme was one of the most extraordinary features of thi3 gigantic fraud. Tho "Societedes Sucreries et Exploitations Agricoles de Port Breton" was the most important of these, because circumstances connected with it are cited as crushing evidence of the bad faith of Du Breil, or the Marquis de Rays, as we had better call him. So far, at worst, the Marquis is only made to appear an enterpiising swindler; but much heavier charges weigh upon him. His speculation in human life

explains the horror which his name excites in France. A colony without colonists was too palpalbe an absurdity even for his clients, consequently it became absolutely necessary at an early stage of his operations to find emigrants for New Ireland. A serious difficult}', however, presented itself. The French Government refused to smile upon the philanthropic work, and put its veto on the Marquis' scheme. Meanwhile he had bought a'sailiDg vessel of llOOtonß—theChandernagor—which with S7 emigrants on board, was preparing to leave Havre for Port Breton, when orders •came forbidding it to Bail. The Marquis, being a man of quick resources, ran up the . American flag and gave the command to an American, MacLaughlin. The whole of the French crew having been withdrawn and foreign sailors substituted, the vessel left under cover of night. The next day the Marquis announced the departure in the following telegram:—"The Chandernagor has left, carrying with her a people's destinies !" The details of the voyage are most distressing, disgustiug' orgies alternating with fierce quarrel a, for the emigrants representing different nationalities, they soon separated into hostile camps. To the utmost license succeeded the most barbarous discipline. The punishment usually resorted to was that of stringing unfortunate wretches up to the mast by their thumbs. Before reaching their destination 14 emigrants were put ashore on one of tfxe Laughnan islands, without provisions, where their sufferings were frightful. Only six of them survived. The Chandernagor reached Port Breton in January, 1880. The emigrants having been put on shore, they were immediately abandoned, and the vessel made for Sydney, where it is stated she was sold for a paltry aum. The number of emigrants landed at Port Breton \yas 67. Having endured every kind of privation, five of them took the boat with the hope of finding a more hospitable inland. They landed on one of the Bourgaicville group, where four of the adventurers •were killed and eaten by the natives. The fifth caved his life by consenting to join in

the hideous banquet upon his dead companions. This man ultimately managed to escape. Of the entire band of emigrants who left in the Chaodernagor 39 returned to France, chiefly through the istervention of English missionaries, 27 died, and 21 disappeared. The second vessel despatched was the Genii. She sailed from Barcelona and embarked 28 Spaniards, described as "la crane de la canaille delaville." She was commanded by Captain Raberdy. The Spaniards fought continuously until Aden was reached, where they were put o. shore and exchanged for Arabs. Soon afterwards the second in command, one Richard, left in a boat with nine others, and they have rot . been heard of since. At Point do. Galle the reat of the Arabs ran away. At Singapore half the crew deserted. When the vessel reached Port Breton there were barley sufficient men on board to navigate her. The third vessel to leave was the India. She started from Barcelona in May, 1880, with 339 emigrants, 250 being Italians. The Italian Consul'tried his utmost to prevent their leaving, but without auccesa. The j Marquis accompanied his "colonists" a few ' miles to sea, then left them with many ; encouraging words. Having returned to ] shore, he despatched the following tjlegram i for the information of those whose mooey he i had obtained " Great victory! Glory be 1

to God. The India has sailed. I conducted her myself to sea. Long live Spain and NewFrance !" Among the passengers in this vessel were many women, old men and children. The India reached Port Breton with few incidents. The emigrants were woefully discouraged by the first impressions of their paradise. They met their fate, however, bravely, and made a desperate effort to found a colony. Semi-starvation soon damped their adour, and those who survived wore glad enough to re*embark in the India of New Caledonia. When they departed 51 victims had been buried at Port Breton. The fourth and last vessel which the Marquis de Bays dospatcbod was the Nouvelle Bretagne, in 1381. She carried the "colonial army"—Bo desperate men who had engaged themselves as gensdarmerie for five years on condition of receiving 15 hectares of land each. The greater portion of this colonial army deserted before Port Breton was reached. Only a handful were landed and they were ultimately sent home after enduring horrible privations. This is a rough sketch of the Marquis de Rays' great colonisation scheme. You can imagine that the first reports that reached Frauce of the emigrants' experiences in New Ireland caused consternation among those who had invested their capital in the undertaking. The mont*y not only ceased to flow into the Marquis' coffers, but the French police began to make enquiries respecting his whereabouts. It would seem that he was prepared for this, for ho had withdrawn completely to Barcelona before the storm broke. Htre he believed himself to bo safe from any hostile ..leasures which his country might take against him. He had, however, left the extradition treaty between France and Spain out of his calculations. He was arrested, together with those who made themselves most conspicuous in his service, and the rest is well known.
