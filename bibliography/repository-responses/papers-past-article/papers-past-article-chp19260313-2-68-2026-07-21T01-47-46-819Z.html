A BLACK UTOPIA FOR NEW ZEALAND.

(SPECTALLT TOITTEN TOB "THE _PSESS.") (By A. J. Harrop.) While searching in the Bibliotheque : Rationale, Paris, for records dealing , with the French project for colonising New Zealand in 1840, I was startled to find there the manifesto, dated 1858, of a "Compagnie Polynesienne" and a "Project for an establishment to be founded by men of colour in New Zealand" (Projet d'un etablissement a fonder par des homines de couleur dans la Nouvelle Zelaude, Oceanic). Ever 6ince its discovery, New Zealand had exercised an attraction for French minds. Captain Cook anticipated de Surville by a very short period of time. Even the terrible fate of Captain Marion Dufresue, who was killed and eaten-by the Maoris, did not deter the French navigators, although it gave them an opinion of the Natives which was' a powerful force in restraining French schemes of colonising New Zealand. French whalerß and sealers willingly ran tho gauntlet of Maori ovens for tho rich reward that awaited them off the New Zealand coasts. A French baron, Charles de Thierry, who, when an undergraduate at Cambridge, had acquired by purchase certain claims to land in New Zealand, formed an ambitious project of a Sovereign Government in New Zealand of which he was to be the head. He proposed, in the year 1535, to facilitate communication with New Zealand by a canal through the Isthmus of Darie'n, and in 1837 he published an "Address to the white peoplo of New Zealand," explaining his philanthropic intentions towards them. The Baron's plans failed, as did those of the Nanto-Bordclaise Company, which was forestalled in an attempt to establish a French colony at Akaroa, in the South Island. After this failure, it has been generally thought that French political interest in hew Zealand died out altogether. This manifesto of 1858, now published in English for the first time, shows that the attraction of New Zealand was still potent. Incidentally, it gives a sidelight on the history of the humanitarian movement for the abolition of slavery. Here is the translation:~"There are some epochs in the history of the world when the thoughts

of all men are turned towards tho 1 future—epochs of solemn expectation. J There has never been an epoch like ours, nor one more fruitful of unexpected events. Everywhere instincts of liberty are , being aroused; all the peoples are uniting. There is not a single echo which does not respond to the voice of to the voice of humanity "Go ahead!' , "The factor which dominates the real history of peoples and races is the>, i need of life and expansion which agi- 1 tates nations and individuals and in- , duces them to mingle with each other. ( Why should not we take an active part " in this movement? 1 "In truth, Africa and Oceania are i cur Far West, they are the countries j of_ our race, the countries of our future. We have resolved to found in Oceania ' a new establishment like that of Liberia, on the coast of Africa. The • example is being imitated; it is a new : landmark on the road of the future; j it is the development of tho same idea on a_ larger base. 1 "We do not wish to limit our work to the interests of politics or to those ; of one nation; it is a work of progress, a humanitarian work, that we wish to undertake. Our association is to comprise not only the English and French colonies, but all European colonies, as much those which have maintained slavery as those which have abolished it, thus reuniting all men of colour without distinction of race or nationality in a common idea: tho creation of a coloured people independent and free. It is a work for the future of a whole race. Therefore, Union and Faith, fo r Union is strength (Ainsi done, Union et Foi, car 1 'union e'est la force). This is the way in which wo expect to work: "There will be successively established in London, Paris, and all the different Colonial centres, Councils of the Polynesian Company, composed of six members, a president, a treasurer, a secretary, and three councillors. . . • (There follow rules for the various officers and tho collection of subscriptions.) "Tho colonists will be divided into two classes: Free colonists, of whatever colour, and voluntary indentured labourers, white, black, or mulatto. The free colonists will travel to tho colony as ordinary passengers. . . . They will receive from the Company's agents concessions of land, according to their choice at 10 francs per hectare. The indentured labourers are those who, being unable to pay their passages, agree to work for a certain time not exceeding three years for the Company, or for free colonists. . . . (Regulations for registering intending colonists here follow.) "The Company's labourers will bo considered as soldiers, amenable to the disciplinary laws of the French Army, and their offences will be dealt with by Councils of War. They will open roads and cultivate land for the benefit of the Company. Working almost always on the frontiers, they will serve as a defence force for tho colony. The lands cultivated will either be worked by the Company or sold by auction to free colonists. "In case of necessity all the 00 hj 11 " ists must serve in the militia, if called upon by the Governor. The governor, assisted by a Colonial Council elected by the colonists for five years, will be the supremo chief of tti© colonv. He will be appointed and subject to recall by the President-General of tho company. He m agreement with his council, organise the administration of the colony, and name the Sheriffs. Deputy-teherittß, .Justices of the Peace, and constables. (Regulations for these officers folloWf) "Tho first care of the administration must always be to protect, as far as possible, the liberty of individual action, according to the American principle which has been in colonisation from tho point of view of rapid development, whatever French opinions may bo on the point We wish to do forthe benefit of our race, and with the moral support of all civili~<*l nations, what the United States have already done, with benefit to American interests in founding Liberia. "We believe that, in the present state of civilisation the whole future of the coloured races can rest only on tho fusion and mixture of peoples, according to the great law of progress. Wo do not wish to exclude, therefore, all whites from our establishments, ae is done in the American colony. On the contrary, we call them in, as a means of progress and activity to counterbalance to a certain extent the native influence of men oft colour in general. But we shall consider them only as assistants, a.s strangers still, until they have contracted an alliance among us, reserving solely to men of colour, black or mulatto,, and to whites united with us, all the administrative duties, in order to create for them that pre-eminence in the special order which is absolutely necessary for the object we have in view. "We dare to hope that for a work of this kind, a work of progress and civilisation, powerful sympathy will not be wanting. We appeal to all men of good will, without distinction of colour ; or race. Let them come to plant with i us, under a new sky, the flag of the . future; let them come to create, with , us, on new shores, a new free people, and proclaim with us, before the whole world, the destiny of- humanity! "Union of the peoples, fusion of the races, progress!" We ask all those who would like to assist us in the realisation of our work to communicate with us. ; Comte du Breil de Rays, Chateau de Quimerch en Bannaleo (Finistere). "January Ist, 1858." To this manifesto was attached a covering letter dated February 4th, 1858, addressed to M. Brisette, former representative of Martinique, appealing for his assistantce in the enterprise. M. Brisette, however, had died on January 22nd, and .the scheme apparently did not long survive him. , Its most extraordinary feature is the fact that it contains no reference to the measures to be taken to secure territory in New Zealand. Although the British Museum contains no copy of the manifesto, it possesses a work which throws much light on the founder of the Polynesian Company, and reveals him as no idle dreamer. The title of the work is "Nouvelle-France, colonie libre de Port-Breton," and the sub-title, "A Work of Colonisation, Agricultural, Christian, and Free, of M. Charles du Breil, Marquis de Rays." It was on April 4th, 1879, that the Marquis held at Marseilles a conference in connexion with his scheme of a free colony in Oceania. In arousing French interest in the Pacific Islands, though he met with fierce opposition in many quarters, he had far more success than in his project of a Black Utopia for New Zea- ' land, the story of which serves to emphasise the close connexion between England and France in the Dominion's history.
