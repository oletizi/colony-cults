THE BROTHERS OF THE CHAIN.

CHAPTER XIII.

' Well, M. Ie Marquis, and now that you have got this refractory brother of yours safely under lock and key, may I ask what you propose to do with him ?' 'My dear Ma'am'selle d'Avranche, to tell you the truth, the miracles ot the last hour or two have happened so rapidly that I have not been able to form any very clear idea, but now that we shall have a little leisure I have no doubt that if you give the marquis and myself the benefit of that keen and practiced intellect of yours there will be very little difficulty in arriving at a satisfactory conclusion.'

' The case is certainly somewhat- complicated,' said the Marquis d'Avranche, lifting his champagne glass from the table and holding it up so that the light from a cluster of electric bulbs over the saloon table danced among the rising bubbles. ' Yes, it is distinctly intricate, but I daresay we shall be able to find a satisfactory solution before we get to the Mediterranean. Dear me, how very steadily this boat goes ! Ot course, the sea is fortunately very smooth, but' still we're travelling at something like forty miles an hour. Really, with this new adaptation of the turbine system there's no vibration at all. It would be rather awkward if we hit anything, Captain Fraiikland, wouldn't it ?'

' Very,' said the tall, loose-limbed young gentleman, who had directed the operations from the bridge of the yacht on this and another memorable occasion far away. «If it waa anything like our own size, we should simply knock it to bits and go through the pieces at express speed, but if we hit anything big and solid say, like the late lamented ' Scandinavian,' for instance, we would simply bore a hole in her and then crumple up and go down with her. And as we have to travel without lights—' ' Thanks, capt-iin,'' interrupted Mile. d'Avrauche, with a. slight shrug of her pretty shoulders. ' Sufficient unto the hour is the evil thereof. Never mind the collision till it comes. - Surely the wide ocean is wide enough for us to get past the other ships somehow, and besides under such guidance as yours surely an accident would be impossible.' The last words were said very prettily, aud George Frankland indulged in a brief but earnest glance across the table at the radiant beauty opposite him, and in that instant thought many things. But in the world under the command of the Lord of the Chain there were social crades just as there were in the other world, and he was to her, despite the fact that both of them were ex-convicts, only as the captain of a roailboat might be to one of his lady passengers in the saloon. For the present they were on terms of equality, but there was a very great gulf fixed between them for all that. Nominally he,was the servant of the Marquis and therefore hers, and that was the end of it.

' It is very kind of you to say that,' he said, ' and of course we shan't hit anything. We are outside the steamer tracks already and there are half a dozen men constantly on the look-out, so there's no fear of our not sighting anything there is about and keeping well out of its way.'

* Very well then,' said Ma'm'selle, ' we can go on discussing our dessert and our plans in peace. I think myself that the best way to do would be to drop our passenger on some convenient island or bit of seashore, where he would be picked up after a suitable interval, and let him fall into the hands of the French police as quick as possible. Of course, the territory would have to be French.'

' Yes,' said the Marquis, ' but I don't think that could be done quite as quickly as you suggest. You see one doesn't bring a man down from his condition to that of an evade from La Nouvelle in a few days, whatever discipline you may give him ; andjthen you know the brand will take some time to heal, and although that preparation which I have applied has a wonderful effect in making it look old, still his appearance is not that of a man who has worked at La Nouvelle. He wants sunburn and—well, other little details that I needn't mention, before he is quite fitted to assume his new role in life.'

' Well,' said Merrivale, ' he's got the irons on, and he'll get his back pretty well pickled to correspond with something like what mine was ' —he gave an expressive litile shrug of the shoulders as he said this—' but I think a week or two's exposure to the sun on such a place as Ma'm'selle suggests ought to complete the treatment. The only question is, where ? What do you think, Marquis ?'

' Let me see, now,' said the Marquis, leaning his elbow, on the table and putting the tips of his fingers together. ' It is really a matter of time and arrangement. We left the Solent seven days ago on a month's cruise to the Mediterranean. We are popularly supposed to steam about twelve knots an hour at sea, and we can rea'lv do thirty-live. Therefore, if we turn up at Nice in a couple of days' time we shall have made about an average passage, and no one would believe that we have been where we have bten. Our passenger can be kept in safety and put through his necessary training meanwhile ; we can cruise about the Mediterranean for a few days and then take a trip down to Suez through the canal.

' After that, having kept our movements respectably pubiic all the time and travelling only at what I may call our respectable speed, we can prolong our trip, say, to Ceylon, which is nothing unusual for a yacht of thia size to do now-a-days. Then, once ciear of civilization in the Red sea, we can run down to the Indian ocean, travelling slowly when we're in sight of anything and at our best speed when we're not, cut across to Northern Australia, land our passenger somewhere in the wilds, and arrive in Ceylon about the time that we should have got there with our everyday speed.' 'That's an excellent plan, my dear papa, as far as it goes,' said Ma'm'selle, 'but I'm afraid it doesn't go quite far enough. You see, when the A.arquia gets back to London he will have to justify his existence, as it were, in his new character. He leaves as Victor Merrivaie, an Englishman of partly French extraction. He goes back as Hector de Meidac, Marquis de Pontarche. How is he going to do that unless the late Marquis, as we may call him, takes his place as the •' evade ' from La Nouvelle recaptured by the French police ?' 'Well,' said Victor, 'I don't think there need be very much difficulty about that. Why not drop him on French territory at once ? I mean after he has received the necessary amount of discipline. Why not Madagascar, for instance, instead of Australia? It would be less out of our way and he would stand a much better chance of being recaptured than he would have in Australia. We could have him up on deck for the benefit of the sun when no one was looking, and he ought to be quite ready by the time we get down there.' ' Ass that I am !' exclaimed the Marquis, suddenly bringing his hand down on the table. 'Ass—not tohave thought of it before.'

'My dear papa, what ia the matter?' asked Ma'm'selle, raising her pretty eyebrows.

' The matter? Are we not going to the Mediterranean, and is not Tunis French territory, and have we not agents there? What is easier ? We can land him there quietly, and hand him over to them. Several of them are in the police. They will know how to harmonise the master's orders with their official duties, they will impose any discipline that they may think necessary, and arrange for his recapture at their leisure.'

' Yes,' objected Merrivale, • but there will have to be a defiuite story, as Ma'm'selle said.'

' What a pity we can't just drop him overboard and have done with it,' said Ma'm'selle, with another shrug of her shoulders. ' I'm afraid this man is going to be rather a nuisance to v..'

' I'm afraid so,' said the Marquis, ' but unfortunately it can't be helped. No, it is quite necessary that our passenger should be handed over to the police when he has acquired his new role, because, you see, if there is to be a Marquis de Pontarche, possessor of our friend's revenues and estates in Paris and well known in London, there must also be a Victor Merrivale, an ' evade' at large, or a convict of La Nouvelle. The one is, a. it were, a complement of the other. What do you think, captain ?' ' I'm afraid I must agree with you, Marquis,' replied Frankland, 'and it doesn't look altogether easy. You see there's the ugly fact that a passenger calling himsel. the Marquis de Pontarche sailed from Capetown in the Scandinavian. Now the Scandinavian is at the bottom of -.he Atlantic, and the Marquis o'e Pontarche is here on board this yacht. The passenger list has been telegraphed Home to England; the Scandivavian will never be Heard of again unless we tell. Now, ho*.v are you g- ing to resuscitate the Marquis de Pontarche without letting out that we know something about the fale of the Scandinavian ?'

' There's only one way out cf that as far as I can see,' said Ma'm'selle. 'We shall have to manage things so as to n:ake. it appear that the person who travelled home by the Scandinavian as the Marquis de Ponarche was an impostor - that he was not the Marquis at all, but some swindler who had taken hid name, since the real Marquis de Pontarche sailed with us on this yacht to find the convict who had been masquerading under his name, and band him over to justice so that he might take his own position again. Of course, our friend Tsernovik would do that, only it is perfectly well known that he was coming home in charge of a detective.

' Ah, yes,' said the Pole, who had so far sat without speaking a word, but pondering every word that had been spoken by the others, ' but if you will pardon me I don't quite see how that will work. You see, even I shall have to keep very quiet for some time — perhaps for years —and do all my work through agents. And there is another danger for you. Suppose I was recognised. It is known that I sailed ou the Scandinavian, with that dear fellow Inspector Frost, who was so kind to me, and whose loss I regret so deeply, and if I were recognised people would ask how I came to be saved from the Scandinavian ? How much the harder, then, to account for M. Ie Marquis !'

' How would it be,' said Frankland, speaking in a slow, deliberate tone, and looking down at the white tablecloth as he spoke, ' how would it be to say that as we were crossing the Bay of Biscay— you know there has been some be-stly bad weather up there lately, and there may be again, as it's just the time of the year for it—well, suppose we say we came across a lot of wreckage, and that we found the superfluous Marquis hanging on to a life buoy or a hen coop, or something of that sort, that we pick him up, and as, of course, he is in the last stages of exhaustion, we bring him on board, strip him, and rub him down, and put him between hot blankets in a berth, and give him hot soup and brandy, and that sort of thing, and then find that he is the living image of the real Marquis there—which of course would lead to questions and then the steward would say that he had found a curious mark on the back of his shoulder : «T. F. 999' in a circle.

" Then our friend the Marquis would recognise him as his long and well-lost brother, and, knowing his previous history, ot course his first duty would be reluctantly to hand him over to the French police, say at Marseilles. How does that strike you ? Of com se, it would be a rather extraordinary coincidence, but if our friend the Marquis here was able to establish his own identity and to compare shoulders with his unfortunate relative, I don't think there ought to be very much difficulty in getting out of it. There's one thing very certain ; the French police would never believe the story of the man who had the braud on him, whatever it was."

' Captain Frankland,' said Ma'm'selle, with one of her most radiant glances, • you are a genius. It is the very solution that was wanted. Wreckage in the Bay of Biscay, on the very homeward track of the Scandinavian, a solitary man clinging to wreckage is saved by us and tells us a story of collision with some unknown ship which went down at the same time. He flings himself into tho sen, swims about, and finds something to which he clings. We, crossing the bay toward Gibraltar, rescue him the next morning, and then, as you say, we find him to be the false Marquis de Pontarche. A wonderful coincidence certainly. But who is going to deny it ? Papa, I think that will do, won't it? '

'Subject to future discussion, for which we shall have plenty of leisure,' said the Marquis : ' I have no doubt it will. Captain Frankland, you have certainly found the best solution to our puzzle, if it is successful, it shall be remembered to your advantage.' The commander of the Sybile bowed, and then Ma'm'selle got up and said : ' Now, it's rather hot down here ; suppose we go up into the deck saloon. I want a blow.'

•And, therefore, perhaps,' said Merrivale, ' you will allow me to offer you my arm, because you will find something like a gale of wind blowing on deck. We are travelling nearly forty miles an hour, you know. '

' Yes,' she said. Isn't it wonderful ? Well, M'sieu' le Marquis, I will take your arm, Come along. When they got up on deck, having left the Marquis, Tsernovik, and the captain to a further discussion over their cigars and liquers, they found the yacht Hying through the smooth water at such a speed that the wind was roaring over the bridge above them, and making the wire stays sing like fiddle-strings. But, thanks to Captain Frankland's forethought, the after part of the deck had been so completely covered in by canvas guards lashed across the deck and along the sides that there was a space of a dozen yards or so that was quite sheltered, and aa windless as though the yacht had been lying quite still on the water.

Ma'm'selle Lena had put on one of her white fluffy wraps which women affect on steamboats after dinner in the tropics, and she looked dangerously beautiful as she ascended the cabin stair_ on Merrivale'-* arm. But on deck all was dark-

ness, as far ns the ship's lights were concerned. All the under deck ports had been carefully shrouded, and on deck there was not a glimmer or light save from the lamps of the binnacle on the bridge shining on the compass card, and aud even these were so covered up that they were invisible to anyone except the officer on the bridge and the man at the wheel.

The stars recked slowly to and fro, high up in the clear heavens, as the Sybile _wung from right to left with the swell. But that was all. For the rest she was a thing shrouded in mystery, rushing through the night as dark and mysterious as the purposes of those for whom her marvellous engines were now working at the topmost speed. ' Now, how pleasant and convenient it might be,' said Lena, as they began their promenade up and down the speltered space, ' if things were only just a little otherwise. For instance, if I were really Lena d'Avranche instead of the daughter of a commissary of police who had got herself into trouble, and you, my dear Victor, were really what our poor patient down below is, Marquis de Pontarche, and—ah, but I foraot the English girl. I suppose you really are honestly, really quite '

' Yes,' lie said, ' J am, and that is just the truth. If I were, as you say, really what I am going to pretend to be, there could only be cne Marquis de Pontarche.'

' And, therefore,' she .aid, with a a rather bitier little laugh, ' therefore, it is ju.t as well that my instructions are to capture Mr Mark Standish and his millions.' 'Indeed !' he said. 'Is that so ?' ' There are the orders,' she said, ' and not very disagreeable ones, either, to obey. He is a very presentable specimen of a man indeed ; ait-iough I can't say that I altogether care about these big, fair-haired, bulldoggy sort of men that the Anglo-Saxon race seems to breed. Still, they are men, and, of course, his millions are irreproachable. Ha ! what's the matter on the bridge ? Let's go and see.'

They mounted the bridge ladder quickly, for by the starlight they had seen the officer put his glasses up to his eyes, then take a couple of quick strides to the helmsman, whisper something in his ear, and then whistle down a tube connecting with the saloon. When they reached the bridge a storm of wind struck them suddenly across the face. Lena's head covering vanished in an instant, and her hair was blown instantly out of its coils and plaits. Merrivale's op also vanished into tbe darkness astern, for a forty-mile wind was sweeping over the top of the canvas guards. Then, shading their eyes with their hands, they made out a great gray shape looming out of the black water ahead to the left. At the same moment the yacht swung round to 'the right. Captain Frankland camerunning bareheaded up onto the bridge. "

' A cruiser—by all that's unlucky ! Confound him ! I hope he won't see us. Going the same way as we are too. Keep her away, Johnson, keep her away. 'Hard over, man ; hard over. Show him your heels.' ' Hard over it is, sir,' replied the helmsman. The next moment there can.c a hail from the cruiser, something about lights came floating across the water, and then a blinding glare, shattering the darkness in an instanr, flashed out, and just for one moment the cruiser's searchlight blazed white and cl-ar on the decks and bridge of the Sybile. The next moment a turn of her helm carried her out of the path of light; they saw the great white, fan-shaped beam wavering about between sky and water, but ie did not touch them again until the Sybile was or.ly a tiny speck in the far distance, and then vanished.

' Confound him ! I hope he isn't a Britisher,' said Captain Frankland to the Marquis, who had followed him on the bridge. 'It would be rather awkward if he went and reported this to Gib., or somewhere like that.'

'My dear captain, we shall be in Toulon long before he is in Gibraltar,' replied the Marquis, 'and he could not possibly identify us in a momentary flash like that.'

* Now, I wonder wh.t the deuce that fellow was,' Senior Lieutenant Alfied Stir nont was saying to himself, about the same time, as he stood on the navigating bridge of Her Majesty's cruiser Minerva, following the sweeping rays of the searchlight with his glasses and trying vainly to pick up the vanished craft again. "Two masts, two funnels; big for her size, big for a yacht—getting on for eight hundred tons, I should say ; graceful lines, lies low, good beam, no lights ; doesu't answer hail ; changed her course when we picked her up, and steams like the very Well, whatever she is, she doesn't look honest. Something a bit out of the common to log, at any rate Confound her ! I wonder what she was.'
